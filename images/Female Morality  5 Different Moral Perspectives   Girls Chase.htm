Female Morality: 5 Different Moral Perspectives

By Alek Rolstad


Morality is a varied field, and we can view women’s morality in quite different ways... depending on which of 5 branches of morality we use.

Note: this article is part of a discussion on female morality among experts who view the subject through different lenses. In this article, Part 3, Alek Rolstad introduces five different moral paradigms that can be used to view female morality.

Hey, guys. I am aware that I don’t usually discuss theoretical stuff that is not directly related to the field, but I decided to take some liberties today.

Recently, Hector Castillo wrote a post on “women not caring about morality” that some of you may have loved, hated, loved to hate, or hated to love. There is no doubt that subjects related to morality may be seen as controversial, triggering a variety of feelings in different people. Chase also weighed in on the subject with his own article in response to the heated debate brewing in Hector’s comments section.

In regards to ethics and the philosophy of morality, there is no such thing as full-blown truth. Ethics is a subfield of philosophy, meaning it is less likely to contain the types of truths you’d find in science – as philosophy is not science. Philosophy is the process of discovering truth, and for this reason, we have decided, in light of good old Socratic tradition, to learn through debate. By presenting multiple takes on the matter (Hector, Chase, and now me – so far), we hope to give you more arguments to fuel your reflections and hopefully contribute to your reaching a (more) solid conclusion – if you ever reach one at all.

What Hector points out (that descriptive ethics is more fact based compared to normative ethics) is very true. However, there is still some normativity within descriptive ethics. As mentioned, descriptive ethics devotes itself more to “how people act” rather than “how people should act” – the latter going into the field of normativity. However, in order to discuss how people actually act (what kind of moral sentiments and drives they possess), we need to define one of the key variables. Namely, what is “morality”? If we want to discuss the observed morality of women, how we define morality will have a key impact on our discussion.

Now, how we define this variable will have a crucial impact on our observations. This is where my critique mainly flourishes.

I will cover my criticism step by step, and like Hector, I will add references to the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, which is considered a very credible source. This way, you can read more on the different theories if you happen to find this interesting.

Before I get to my arguments, I do want to make it deadly clear that there are no rights and wrongs – only good arguments. Hopefully, my arguments will be as convincing as Hector’s and, in the end, help you solidify you own.


The Observations

Descriptive ethics, as covered by Hector, discusses “observed moral behavior.” This entails observations. Now, it is not Hector’s job to perform a “Big N” quantitative analysis and spot correlations. And secondly, such a quantitative analysis would be prone to epistemological limitations.


Methodological Limitations

First of all, we are somewhat limited to observing only the actions of other people – in this case, women – as we’re unable to truly analyze the motives and intentions behind their actions. This is simply not doable with a quantitative analysis.

Now, the motivations of women can be analyzed better through qualitative analysis – i.e., interacting directly with many women over a long period. This is where both Hector and I get our data, and our data is equally legit as a quantitative analysis; however, it is bound to suffer from reliability problems. The observations are valid, but others may not be able to reproduce similar observations. Truth is, my observations do differ a bit from Hector’s.

I am not saying that Hector’s observations are wrong, but I don’t believe they are valid for everyone (hence the reliability issues), as the “female” variable is too broad, which results in different observations based on which types of women we interact with, where, and in what situations predominantly. However, I would have been careful making universal claims regarding female morality.

And believe me, ethnic differences do play a role, but we do not need to go any further than simply meeting a girl from a different sub-culture, different stereotype, or different personality to notice a huge change in behavior.


Women from different races, cultures, sub-cultures, and personalities can all behave very differently.

Biological Drives

Hector covers in many of his posts that certain biological drives, such as sexual and reproductive drives, affect our behavior, including our moral compass. This is absolutely true. Our behaviors are affected by our sexuality and reproductive needs and desires. And this is the main subject of Hector’s post.

He dedicates a huge portion of his article on this, as it was one of the main premises of his argument. As I do happen to agree with his descriptive premise and find his coverage of the subject to be more than satisfying, I won’t dedicate too much time discussing that. However, the way he uses the premise to reach his conclusion is where I would disagree.

There are mainly two things I disagree with:

  1. One of his main conclusions says that women are amoral because they are mostly driven by their sexual strategy. Although it is true that they are driven by their sexual strategy, the same could be said for men. As Hector points out, men have created art, built amazing buildings, conquered lands, and have gone to war for women. No doubt men are also driven by their sexual strategy.

  2. In order for women to be amoral, there needs to be either something amoral about living according to their instinctive drives, or there needs to be something transcendental (something that goes beyond) about males that makes males moral and women amoral.

I will leave the first point open to discussion. I believe a lot of the socially-constructed moral conventions related to loyalty, matrimony, and sexuality are not based on any transcendental a priori principles (i.e., rational principles that go beyond our mere physical existence) but are rather defined as a result of feelings (such as emotional bias) and/or utilitarian principles (utilitarianism being the moral theory stating that rightness is that which leads to the most utility). A lot of these ideas were covered in one of my first posts on the secret society where I discussed the rise of the “sexual order” through the use of conventions and artificial virtues.

In the next section, I will discuss the second point in more depth: is there something about women that goes beyond their mere instincts related to sexual strategy? Or is there something lacking?


Woman and Morality

The discussion of what morality is has historically been a discussion covered by men, as women were historically denied access to education. Only recently with the feminist movement (the first wave and the second wave), women have joined the discussion.

For this reason, a lot of our notions of what morality is have been defined by men, and some of the contributions have been really good. But in contrast, a perspective dealing with the whole of humanity is lacking when one sex is absent from the debate. And as you may guess, women present a very different view on what morality is.


What she views as moral is probably quite different from what you do.

That said, I will skip the debate on what morality is. As a matter of fact, I haven’t truly made up my own mind. Just like Hector, I will drop the discussion related to normative ethics and stick to descriptive ethics. As mentioned earlier, it is hard to get into descriptive ethics without at least indirectly bringing in normativity.

Either way, let us briefly discuss ethics and female critics.

The theories covered below will only be addressed briefly. The coverage of moral theories will serve primarily to further my point that women have dispositions in light of the most common normative theories.

If women are amoral, it follows that women would lack the dispositions to act according to normative moral theories. I will therefore use some examples and see whether or not they fit with typical female dispositions. Again, the theories covered will just serve as examples to illustrate a point, not to provide a full breakdown of each theory. In fact, I will present a very simplified version of each theory.


The Ethics of Caring

Here is an in-depth resource on this subject if you wish to dig deeper.

The major criticism of female scholars (one of the most prolific being Carol Gilligan) was based on the lack of focus on emotions in the ethical debates. The ethics of care is actually one of the main ethics taught in nursing. It is about caring for one another – showing compassion, maintaining good relationships.

Classical ethical theories focus on universal principles. Different people live in different situations, and context matters. Contextual variables that are taken into consideration are:

  • The relationships and the varying degree of dependence and interdependence between actors

  • Those most vulnerable to one’s choice require the most attention (recall the situation with girls not wanting to make out with me in front of orbiters?)

  • Promoting good relationships between the actors

As we can see, a lot of these principles are based on biology and the psychology of women. Yes, biology matters and will affect the way we perceive ethics. But female ethics merely differing from typical male ethics does not make women necessarily less moral. We are wired differently for different purposes in life. Therefore, it is only logical that the notions of right and wrong differ drastically between the sexes.

Here we clearly see that promoting good social relationships between actors can be tied to female survival instincts, and caring for the most vulnerable is tied to motherly instincts.


Sentimentalism

As with the previous section, I’ll provide a useful academic resource for further study.

Presented originally by David Hume was an emphasis on how emotions drive us morally; we cannot perceive morality, and our rationality is merely the slave of our passions. We can therefore not define what is right, we can only feel what is right.

Hume’s argument basically boils down to “what feels right, is right.” However, in order to avoid total relativism, he advocated the notion of artificial virtues – constructed norms that have been agreed upon by society as a whole. Breaking the norm would thus feel bad, resulting in people avoiding actions outside that norm.


Sentimentalism: if it feels right, it is right.

Additionally, in a similar light, he advocates the use of an imaginary, impartial judge (a moral “conscience”?) to use as a reference point before acting.

Either way, if such a moral theory holds true (that we are driven by moral sentiments), it follows that women also have a sense of morality. If this is not true, the aspect of agency related to women falls as this entails a lack of cognition. This is simply not the case.

Furthermore, in light of artificial virtues, things like slut-shaming do have negative effects on women, and when artificial virtues are enforced, women tend to follow them more strictly than men, as women tend to be more comforting than men.


Virtue Ethics

In similar light, we can analyze the ethics of virtue. Virtue ethics doesn’t tell us much about what we ought to do, but rather what type of person we ought to become and how to cultivate virtues. It is more about the process of how to become a virtuous person. It also covers a lot about what virtues one ought to have.

Traditionally developed by Plato and Aristotle, virtue ethics covers only virtues that could be seen as male virtues – courage, independence, resilience, and so on. Female virtues, however, are mostly left out (virtues such as caring and compassion). Just because these female virtues are left out does not mean women are amoral. They just have different virtues than men. Women are not men, nor should they ever be. Why do they have to follow the exact same moral codes as we would? And to make that clear, this does not give them a free pass to moral nihilism.

But the difference in virtues can be related either directly or indirectly to the biological differences between the sexes.

Hence, if women are dictated by their reproductive strategy and other biological drives, it surely will have an impact on their moral behavior. But it does not make that behavior amoral.


Deontology

This is basically the moral theory (more info) that claims there are some overarching moral principles that are universally true in all cases (a limitation in my opinion). In many cases, these principles are seen as limitations in regards to freedom, as in a means to limit individuals in order to preserve autonomy.

The idea here is (basically) “do whatever you want, but don’t steal, lie, or kill.” These moral theories are usually derived through rational reflections (a priori) in order to grant them universality. An issue with this theory is that it requires a very significant metaphysical foundation.

Being based on logically derived notions and metaphysics – both fields seen as generally masculine – the result is an ethic that is seen as rather unfeminine. Again, with universal principles, context does not matter, personal relationships do not matter, and caring (which is based on sentiments) is not based on mere rationality.

Within this theory, rationality plays a simple role. Female critics have claimed that this “rationality” is primarily based on male ideals of rationality. Whether this is true is up to debate. I personally find deontology very interesting despite kind of disagreeing with it.

If you believe in women not being moral as a result of not having any overlying moral principles that transcend their emotions and biological drives, then your perception seems to fit very well with a deontological approach to ethics. That is fair, although most deontologists refrain from absolutism.

Deontology does not allow for our biological drives to interfere in our moral decisions. Women are driven by emotions, no doubt, which is a true limitation to this theory. However, so are men, and this theory is idealistic, saying we should strive to act beyond our emotions. Now, whether women share that ideal is up to debate. Some might, but it is key to point out that this theory has been the most criticized by most female scholars.

It is said that one of the most prolific deontologists (if not the founder himself), Immanuel Kant, died a virgin. I really love Kant. He was one of the thinkers I have applied the most during my philosophy studies (and my master thesis).


Utilitarianism

This theory, originally developed by Jeremy Bentham, is probably the simplest to explain with few words, but it presents a lot of philosophical issues. Utilitarianism, compared to deontology and virtue ethics, focuses more on the consequences of actions rather than one’s personal disposition and intentions. Now, of course, personal dispositions will have an effect on one’s actions; however, they do not constitute in our judgment of whether an action is good or bad. The action is judged by the action’s consequence (which is why utilitarianism is often referred to as consequentialism).

Now, what is a positive or good consequence? That is something prone to debate, which I will not cover here (there are some good and bad arguments for what a good consequence is). And different forms of utilitarianism have presented different arguments.

So, according to this theory, the perceived “good consequence” may differ between men and women? I mean, this is not an absurd claim; we have different goals and motives, so it follows that our perceptions of what a good consequence is may differ.

One can, for instance, argue that female mating strategies are morally justifiable, as they promote the survival of our species (and so do male mating strategies). Getting the best genetics through mating with the best men and securing providers to secure themselves and their offspring are all good consequences on a macro level. Note that according to utilitarian principles, an action is good if it promotes the most positive consequence for most people. Considering the macro level is therefore key to this theory. Doesn’t matter if a poor guy gets cucked and heartbroken; if the action is good for the species, then so be it – the action is good.


Utilitarianism: if the consequence is good (e.g., for the species), the action itself is good.

You may agree or disagree (or both!) with this. However, there is a clear argument for why the female mating strategy in itself may be positive. Now, by this I will avoid what is called the “naturalistic fallacies” (namely, to claim that “what is” equals “what ought”). But in this case, there seems to be an argument (which you also may or may not agree with) for a harmony.


Conclusion

Again, in ethics, there is no truth, only good arguments. This article presented mine, which you hopefully found good. And Hector has presented his. I know that he has a background in philosophy, hence I found it right to give him the privilege of a good ol’ debate. It’s great that Chase has chimed in as well.

This post is not meant to be a debunking article by any means, just a different perspective based on a different philosophy and different observations. The world is vast, an even here at Girls Chase, we happen to have some different personal values while having different personal experiences (with women), and I believe this to be something positive for the overall community here, which I happen to consider more of a think-tank than anything else.

So, what is the takeaway here?

Well, my takeaway is that there are different views held among the highly-successful dating experts of the world. Each has his own style. My advice to you is to read all three posts, compare them, and use them in your own reflections. I believe it is a good thing in itself to reflect on women and who they are on a moral level. Be open minded, and hopefully you will, in the long run, develop your own views on the matter.

That is the goal.

I hope you enjoyed this article.
